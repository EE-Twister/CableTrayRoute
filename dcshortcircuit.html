DC Short-Circuit & Arc Flash

Method & Assumptions

DC fault current follows IEEE 946-2004 (Recommended Practice for the Design of DC Auxiliary Power Systems for Generating Stations) and IEC 61660-1:1997 (Short-circuit currents in DC auxiliary installations). The arc flash calculation uses the Ammerman & Wilkins (2010) method referenced in NFPA 70E-2024 Annex D.8.1.

Bolted DC fault current (IEEE 946):

Ibf = Voc / Rtotal   where  Rtotal = Rbattery + Rcable + Rbus

L/R time constant:

τ (ms) = Ltotal (mH) / Rtotal (Ω)

For most DC distribution systems, L is negligible and τ ≈ 0. Provide circuit inductance only when it is known from the equipment data sheets.

DC arcing current (Stokes–Oppenlander arc voltage model):

Varc = 20 + 0.534 × g (mm) × Iarc0.12
Iarc = (Voc − Varc) / Rtotal   [solved iteratively]

Reference: Stokes & Oppenlander, "Electric Arcs in Open Air," J. Phys. D: Appl. Phys. 24 (1991) 26–35; Ammerman et al., "DC Arc Models and Incident Energy Calculations," IEEE Trans. Ind. Appl. 46(5), 2010.

Incident energy (NFPA 70E Annex D.8.1 — Lee/Ammerman method):

E = (4.184 × Cf × Parc × tarc) / (2π × Dcm2)   [cal/cm²]

where Parc = Varc × Iarc (W), Cf = 1.0 (open air) or 2.0 (enclosed box), and D is the working distance in cm.

Arc flash boundary (onset of second-degree burn, 1.2 cal/cm²):

Daf = √((4.184 × Cf × Parc × tarc) / (2π × 1.2))   [cm → mm]

Citations: DC Short-Circuit & Arc Flash Guide. See also: Battery / UPS Sizing, AC Short Circuit, AC Arc Flash.

DC Source Parameters

Battery chemistry determines the open-circuit voltage per cell. Lead-acid is standard for DC station batteries (ANSI/IEEE 485).

The number of cells connected in series determines the string voltage. A 48 V lead-acid string uses 24 cells (24 × 2.0 V = 48 V). A 125 V string uses 60 cells; a 250 V string uses 120 cells.

Open-circuit (no-load) voltage of the DC source. Auto-populated when you enter the cell count and chemistry above. Common values: 24 V, 48 V, 125 V, 250 V. Use the nameplate voltage for PV combiner boxes or rectifier outputs.

Total DC internal resistance of the battery string in milliohms (mΩ). For lead-acid cells: typically 0.2–2 mΩ/cell; multiply by cell count for the string. Obtain from the manufacturer's data sheet (the IEEE 485 short-circuit model uses the published internal resistance at the fully charged state).

Circuit Impedance

One-way resistance of the cable from the battery to the fault point in milliohms. Use the Auto-Size tool to calculate cable resistance from conductor size and length. For NEC §240.21, the fault point is typically at the overcurrent device or load terminals.

Resistance of busbars, connections, and terminations in milliohms. Typically 0.5–5 mΩ for short copper bus runs. Leave at 0 for conservative (maximum fault current) analysis.

Total loop inductance of the DC circuit in millihenries. Used only to compute the L/R time constant τ. In most low-voltage DC distribution systems, inductance is negligible (< 0.1 mH) and can be left at 0. Inductance matters for fault current calculations in large DC switchgear or long cable runs.

DC Arc Flash Analysis

Time from arc initiation to clearing (ms). Use the upstream fuse or breaker clearing time at the available fault current from the time-current characteristic. Current-limiting fuses typically clear in < 8 ms. Molded-case circuit breakers may take 50–200 ms.

Distance between electrodes (conductors) in mm. Typical values per NFPA 70E: 13 mm (MCC / switchboard buswork), 25 mm (panel / switchboard terminals), 50 mm (open busbars). Larger gaps generally reduce arcing current.

Distance from the arc source to the worker's face/chest in mm. Default 455 mm (18 in) per NFPA 70E for 240 V panels. Use 610 mm (24 in) for MCC / switchgear; 900 mm (36 in) for switchyard work. Document the actual working distance for the arc flash label.

Enclosed equipment concentrates arc energy (correction factor Cf = 2.0). Open buswork or outdoor equipment uses Cf = 1.0.

DC Short-Circuit & Arc Flash Help

This tool calculates DC fault currents and arc flash incident energy for battery rooms, PV combiner boxes, UPS systems, and DC switchgear per IEEE 946-2004, IEC 61660-1, and NFPA 70E-2024 Annex D.8.1.

Workflow

  1. Enter the battery chemistry and number of cells (or a direct DC bus voltage).
  2. Enter the total battery internal resistance from the manufacturer's data sheet.
  3. Enter the cable and busbar resistance from the source to the fault point.
  4. Enable DC Arc Flash Analysis and enter the protection clearing time, electrode gap, and working distance.
  5. Optionally add protection devices to check interrupt ratings.
  6. Click Run Analysis to see the results.
