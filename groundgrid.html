Ground Grid / Substation Grounding Analysis

Method & Assumptions

Ground grid analysis follows IEEE 80-2013 — IEEE Guide for Safety in AC Substation Grounding. The tool evaluates a rectangular grid of horizontal conductors buried at uniform depth.

Grid resistance (Sverak, IEEE 80-2013 Eq. 57):

Rg = ρ/L + ρ/√(20A) × (1 + 1/(1 + h×√(20/A)))

Mesh voltage (IEEE 80-2013 Eq. 86):

Em = ρ × Ig × Km × Ki / L

Step voltage (IEEE 80-2013 Eq. 92):

Es = ρ × Ig × Ks × Ki / L

Ground potential rise:

GPR = Ig × Rg

Tolerable touch voltage (70 kg person, IEEE 80-2013 Eq. 33):

Etouch70 = (1000 + 1.5·Cs·ρs) × 0.157 / √tf

Tolerable step voltage (70 kg person, IEEE 80-2013 Eq. 30):

Estep70 = (1000 + 6·Cs·ρs) × 0.157 / √tf

Where Cs is the surface layer reduction factor per IEEE 80-2013 Eq. 27, accounting for a high-resistivity surface material (e.g. crushed stone) that reduces the shock current through a person's feet.

Citations: IEEE 80-2013, IEEE 81-2012. See also: Arc Flash, Short Circuit.

Soil Parameters

Measured by Wenner four-pin method (IEEE 81). Typical values: sand 200–2000, clay 10–100, rock 1000–10000 Ω·m. Use the lowest seasonal value for conservative design.

Grid Geometry

Length of the grid in the x-direction (perimeter dimension).

Width of the grid in the y-direction (perimeter dimension).

Number of horizontal runs parallel to the x-axis. Spacing = Ly / (nx−1). A typical square grid has equal nx and ny.

Number of horizontal runs parallel to the y-axis. Spacing = Lx / (ny−1).

Depth of grid conductors below grade. Per IEEE 80-2013: typically 0.5 m (1.5 ft) minimum; deeper burial lowers Rg.

Diameter of the buried conductor. 2/0 AWG Cu ≈ 0.418 in, 4/0 AWG ≈ 0.528 in, 250 kcmil ≈ 0.575 in.

When checked, uses the Kii correction in the mesh factor Km for grids with rods at perimeter corners (IEEE 80-2013 §16.5).

Electrical Parameters

Maximum symmetrical grid current = split factor × 3I₀ (zero-sequence fault current). Obtain from the Short Circuit study. For a conservative design, use the maximum available fault current without applying a split factor (Sf = 1).

Duration of the fault before protective device clears. Typical values: primary relay + breaker: 0.1–0.5 s; backup clearing: 0.5–1.0 s.

IEEE 80-2013 provides limits for both 50 kg and 70 kg body weights. 70 kg is used for most utility substations; 50 kg for conservative designs.

Surface Layer (optional)

A high-resistivity surface material (e.g. 3–6 in crushed stone) raises the tolerable touch and step voltages, improving safety margins significantly.

Resistivity of surface material. Crushed granite: 2000–5000 Ω·m; asphalt: 10,000–2,000,000 Ω·m; concrete: 10,000–1,000,000 Ω·m. Leave 0 if no surface layer.

Thickness of the surface material. Typical crushed stone layer: 0.1–0.2 m (4–6 in).

Ground Grid Analysis Help

This tool calculates substation grounding safety per IEEE 80-2013 for a rectangular grid of buried conductors.

Workflow

  1. Enter soil resistivity from field measurements (IEEE 81 Wenner method).
  2. Define grid geometry: length, width, conductor count, burial depth.
  3. Enter the maximum grid current from the Short Circuit study, applying a current split factor if overhead ground wires divert current away from the grid.
  4. Optionally add a surface layer (crushed stone improves safety margins).
  5. Click Analyze Ground Grid and review mesh and step voltage results against IEEE 80-2013 tolerable limits.

If mesh or step voltages fail, consider: increasing grid conductor density, adding ground rods, enlarging the grid area, or adding crushed stone surface.
