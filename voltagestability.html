Voltage Stability

Method & Assumptions

This study uses a simplified two-bus Thevenin equivalent to trace the steady-state voltage stability limit. The model couples a source bus (Vs = 1.0 pu) to a load bus through a series impedance Z = R + jX (derived from the short-circuit MVA and X/R ratio).

V⁴ + (2PR + 2QX − 1)V² + (P² + Q²)Z² = 0

P-V curve: sweeps active power loading λ from 0 to the nose point. The upper branch is the stable operating region; the lower branch is unstable. The nose (voltage collapse point) gives the maximum transferable power and the MW stability margin.

Q-V curve: sweeps bus voltage from 1.10 pu to 0.30 pu at constant active load and computes the reactive compensation Qcomp needed to hold each voltage level. The minimum Qcomp on the upper branch defines the reactive (Q) margin — how much reactive support can be withdrawn before voltage collapse.

The Thevenin impedance Zpu = Vbase² / (Ssc · Zbase) = MVAbase / MVAsc. Split by X/R: R = Z / √(1 + XR²), X = Z · XR / √(1 + XR²).

This model is appropriate for preliminary stability screening of radial feeders, industrial plant main buses, and DER interconnection points. Multi-bus systems with voltage regulators, generator reactive limits, or switched capacitors require a full continuation power flow (CPF) solver.

Reference: P. Kundur, Power System Stability and Control, Chapter 14; IEEE/PES Task Force, Voltage Stability Assessment, 2002.

Voltage Stability Help

This study generates P-V and Q-V curves to assess steady-state voltage stability margins for a bus represented by a Thevenin equivalent circuit.

P-V curve interpretation

  • Upper branch — stable operating region (normal operation).
  • Lower branch — unstable region (below the nose; voltage regulators may not restore voltage).
  • Nose point — maximum transferable power. Loading beyond this point causes voltage collapse.
  • MW margin — additional load that can be served before collapse. Target: > 20–25% of base case for transmission, > 10% for distribution.

Q-V curve interpretation

  • The curve shows reactive compensation Qcomp (MVAr) needed to hold each voltage level at constant active load.
  • The operating point (Qcomp = 0) corresponds to the base-case voltage with no external reactive compensation.
  • Q-margin — reactive support that can be lost before voltage collapse. Negative Q-margin means the system needs reactive injection to maintain stable operation.

Typical action thresholds

  • MW margin < 15%: consider adding generation or reactive compensation.
  • Q-margin < 0: capacitor bank or SVC required at the bus.
  • Nose voltage < 0.70 pu: bus is heavily stressed; voltage collapse risk is high under contingency.
