Quasi-Dynamic Load Flow

Method & Assumptions

The quasi-dynamic study runs the full Newton-Raphson AC load-flow solver at each timestep in a demand profile. Each step is solved independently (no inter-step dynamics or ramp limits). The result is a time-series of operating points from which voltage envelopes, peak/valley snapshots, and energy losses are derived.

Timestep model:

Pd_t = Pd_base × loadScale_t
Pg_t = Pg_base × genScale_t

All bus loads and shunt elements are scaled uniformly. Generation dispatch is also scaled uniformly — for economic dispatch with generator cost curves use the OPF study.

Voltage limits (ANSI C84.1 Range A):

  • Upper limit: 1.05 pu — fail above this; warn above 1.03 pu
  • Lower limit: 0.95 pu — fail below this; warn below 0.97 pu

Profile format (CSV or text): One row per timestep. Accepted layouts:

  • 1 column: loadScale — hours are assigned automatically (0, 1, 2 …)
  • 2 columns: hour, loadScale
  • 3 columns: hour, loadScale, genScale

Lines beginning with # and header rows starting with "hour" are skipped. Values are per-unit multipliers on the base-case load/generation in the one-line diagram.

Energy loss: Each timestep is assumed to represent 1 hour. Total energy loss (kWh) is the sum of branch losses across all converged timesteps.

References: IEEE Std 399-1997 §14; IEC 60364-8-1:2019 §8 (energy efficiency); CIGRÉ TB 577 (quasi-dynamic simulation of active distribution networks).

Quasi-Dynamic Load Flow Help

The quasi-dynamic study sweeps a time-varying demand profile through the AC load-flow solver to show how bus voltages and branch flows change across the operating day or year.

When to use

  • Annual energy loss estimation for efficiency reporting (IEC 60364-8-1).
  • DER hosting-capacity studies — how much PV/BESS can be connected before voltages violate limits at any hour of the year?
  • Peak/off-peak operating point comparison — which buses are stressed at peak load and which are over-voltage at minimum load?
  • Battery dispatch planning — identify hours where network losses are highest.
  • NERC TPL-001 and IEC 60909 contingency screening over multiple loading conditions.

Profile setup

  • Select a built-in 24-hour or 8760-hour representative profile for quick results.
  • Upload a CSV from your energy meter, SCADA historian, or building energy model.
  • loadScale = 1.0 means full base-case load; 0.5 means 50% of base-case load.
  • genScale applies the same scaling to all generators (useful for curtailment studies).

Interpreting results

  • Voltage envelope: The maximum and minimum pu voltage seen at each bus across all converged timesteps. Buses with max > 1.05 or min < 0.95 need corrective action.
  • Peak snapshot: The timestep with highest total system load — worst-case for undervoltage and thermal overload.
  • Valley snapshot: The timestep with lowest total load — worst-case for overvoltage (especially with embedded DER).
  • Load factor: Average load ÷ peak load. A load factor close to 1.0 means a flat demand profile; close to 0.5 means highly peaky.

Limitations

  • Each timestep is solved independently — no inertia, ramp limits, or inter-period energy storage dispatch.
  • Uniform load scaling: all buses scale together. For bus-by-bus profiles, provide a custom model via the API.
  • Designed for radial and meshed distribution/sub-transmission networks up to ~200 buses.
