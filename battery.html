Battery / UPS Sizing

Method & Assumptions

Battery sizing follows IEEE 485-2010 (lead-acid) and IEEE 1115-2000 (nickel-cadmium). Five sequential de-rating factors are applied to the net energy requirement.

Step 1 — Net energy:

kWh_net = P_avg_kW × t_hours   [uniform load]
kWh_net = Σ(P_i × Δt_i)       [multi-period duty cycle]

Step 2 — Efficiency & depth of discharge:

kWh_design = kWh_net / (η × DoD)

Step 3 — Temperature correction (IEEE 485 §5.2):

K_temp = min(1.0,  1 + coeff × (T_amb − 25°C))
kWh_temp = kWh_design / K_temp

K_temp is capped at 1.0 above 25 °C — IEEE 485 does not credit extra capacity at elevated temperatures.

Step 4 — Aging factor (IEEE 485 §5.3):

kWh_aged = kWh_temp × aging_factor   [1.25 lead-acid, 1.20 Li-ion/NiCd]

Step 5 — Design margin (IEEE 485 §5.4):

kWh_final = kWh_aged × (1 + margin%)

UPS kVA:

kVA_required = P_peak_kW / PF_UPS

Citations: Battery / UPS Sizing Guide. See also: Load List, Load Flow, Capacitor Bank.

System Identification (Optional)

Descriptive label for the UPS or battery system being sized. Used in the results summary only.

Load Parameters

The sustained load the battery must support for the full required runtime. Obtain from the Load List or Load Flow study (P column at the UPS bus). Use the running load, not motor starting peaks — motor starts are short transients.

Maximum instantaneous load, including motor starting transients. Used to determine the minimum UPS kVA rating. Must be ≥ average load. Obtain from the Motor Start study if large motors are present.

Duration the battery must sustain the load after utility power loss. NEC 700 emergency systems require a minimum of 1.5 hours. Data centres typically specify 4–8 hours to allow for generator dispatch or controlled shutdown.

Battery Chemistry & Environment

Valve-regulated lead-acid (VRLA / AGM) is the most common choice for UPS applications. Lithium-Ion provides higher energy density and longer cycle life at higher initial cost. Flooded lead-acid offers low cost for large telecom and substation installations. NiCd is preferred in extreme cold (to −40 °C) and for long float service.

Battery room or enclosure operating temperature. IEEE 485 uses 25 °C as the reference; capacity decreases below this temperature (see IEEE 485 §5.2). Use the lowest expected ambient for worst-case sizing.

Sizing Factors

IEEE 485 §5.4 recommends a minimum 10% design margin for general applications. Use 15–25% for life-safety, mission-critical, or high-uncertainty loads. This margin is applied after the aging factor.

The power factor at which the UPS inverter is rated. Modern double-conversion UPS units are typically rated at 0.9 or 1.0 power factor. Check the manufacturer data sheet — using an incorrect PF will result in an undersized UPS selection.

Battery / UPS Sizing Help

This tool sizes a stationary battery bank and UPS per IEEE 485-2010 (lead-acid) and IEEE 1115-2000 (nickel-cadmium).

Workflow

  1. Enter the average and peak load demand. The average load determines energy; the peak load determines UPS kVA. Obtain from the Load List or Load Flow study.
  2. Set the required runtime. NEC 700 emergency systems require at least 1.5 hours.
  3. Select battery chemistry. Lead-Acid AGM is most common. Lithium-Ion offers higher energy density. NiCd excels in extreme cold.
  4. Enter the battery room ambient temperature. IEEE 485 de-rates capacity below 25 °C.
  5. Set the design margin (minimum 10% per IEEE 485 §5.4) and UPS power factor.
  6. Click Run Analysis to see the required bank size and UPS kVA.

Interpreting results

  • Energy chain — each step showing how the net energy is de-rated to the final installed bank requirement.
  • Recommended bank size — nearest standard kWh rating that meets or exceeds the final requirement.
  • Runtime curve — available runtime at 25%, 50%, 75%, 100%, and 125% of the specified average load. Use this to evaluate load shedding strategies.
  • UPS kVA — minimum UPS inverter rating based on peak load and output power factor. Select the next-larger standard size from the vendor.
