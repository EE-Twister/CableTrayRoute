Tray Hardware BOM & Take-Off

Method & Assumptions

Tray geometry is analyzed to detect junctions between adjacent tray segments. Each junction is classified as an elbow, tee, cross, reducer, or splice plate based on the number of trays meeting and the angle between them.

  • Splice plate — two collinear trays of equal width (bend ≤ 10°)
  • Reducer — two collinear trays of different widths
  • Elbow — two trays at an angle (> 10°)
  • Tee — three trays meeting at a point
  • Cross — four trays meeting at a point

Straight sections are counted using a standard section length (default 12 ft per NEMA VE 2 §3.4). Support brackets are calculated so that no span between supports exceeds the NEMA VE 1 maximum for the selected load class, per the L/100 deflection criterion.

References: NEMA VE 1-2017 §4 (Load Classification), NEMA VE 2 (Installation Guidelines).

See also: Support Span Calculator, Raceway Schedule, Optimal Route.

BOM Options

Standard straight sections ship in 10 ft or 12 ft lengths. Use 12 ft for most steel/aluminum ladder tray; 10 ft for wire-mesh or fiberglass.

Tray Hardware BOM Help

This tool generates a bill of materials for all cable tray hardware — fittings, straight sections, covers, and support brackets — from the tray geometry in your Raceway Schedule.

Load from Project: reads tray data saved in this browser session from the Raceway Schedule page.

Import Raceway XLSX: import the .xlsx file exported from the Raceway Schedule page to use tray geometry from a saved file.

NEMA Load Class controls the maximum support span used to calculate bracket quantities. Class 16A (16 lbs/ft rated) is typical for industrial cable tray.

Export BOM: downloads an Excel workbook with four sheets — Procurement Summary, Fittings, Sections, and Supports.
